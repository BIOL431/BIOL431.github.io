4  Simulation and Probability

Schedule

  • Simulating data (15 min)
  • Functions (10 min)
  • Writing functions (25 min)
  • Basics of probability distributions (20 min)
  • p-values (20 min)

Learning outcomes

After this lecture, students should know:

  • Simulating data can help us understand what assumptions we’ve made and lets us build and test models when we know the answer
  • Functions are a set of code that take inputs and return outputs based on a set of instructions that the user gives
  • Probability distributions can be discrete or continuous
  • Probability distributions have different ranges (sets of possible values) and supports (values they can take)
  • Discrete probability distributions have probability mass, and those probabilities must sum to 1
  • Continuous probability distributions have probability density under each potential value and the area under the curve must sum to 1
  • The normal (Gaussian) distribution has a range of \(\in (-\infty, \infty)\) and support of all real numbers
  • The Bernoulli distribution has a range of \(\in (0, 1)\) and a support of \((0,1)\)
  • The probability mass function for the Bernoulli distribution is \(P(X=1) = p, P(X=0) = 1-p\)
  • Real numbers are those found on the number line (rational and irrational numbers) but not imaginary; integers are whole numbers that can be positive, negative, or zero; rational numbers can be expressed as ratio of two integers; irrational numbers cannot
  • Empirical data can be used to estimate probability of observing data
  • Probability density or mass functions can be used to estimate the probability of observing data
  • A p-value is the probability of observing your data, or data more extreme, if the null is true
