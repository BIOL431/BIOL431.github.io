Hypotheses and models

Schedule

  • Hypothesis framing (20 min), including workshopping hypotheses
  • What is a model (15 min)
  • Rectilinear Pictionary (30 min)
  • The history of statistics and eugenics (15 min)
  • Approaches to inference (10 min)

Learning outcomes

After this lecture, students should know:

  • A hypothesis is a statement about how you think the world works
  • A prediction is what you would expect to observe if your hypothesis is true
  • Predictions let us test hypotheses to make inference about the world
  • Research questions and hypotheses should be grounded in biological reality, not by data or modeling constraints
  • Knowing how to code well enables you to ask, and answer, (almost) any question in ecology and evolutionary biology
  • Models are simplified representations of the real world
  • Models can be represented verbally, graphically, with equations, and in code; modelers should be able to translate between these different formats
  • Adding complexity does not always result in a better model, and parsimonious models are favorable to unnecessarily complex ones
  • Much of modern statistics is rooted in eugenics

Why are we playing Rectilinear Pictionary? Because it forces abstraction, breaks difficult concepts down into simplified components, requires physically drawing a model, lets us embrace ambiguity, and gets us all more comfortable being terribly wrong

  • Optional: Hippopotamus

  • Random variables can be described mathematically

Simulation and probability

Schedule

  • Simulating data (45 min)
  • Basics of probability distributions (25 min)
  • p-values (20 min)

Learning outcomes

After this lecture, students should know:

  • Simulating data can help us understand what assumptions we’ve made and lets us build and test models when we know the answer
  • Functions are a set of code that take inputs and return outputs based on a set of instructions that the user gives
  • Probability distributions can be discrete or continuous
  • Probability distributions have different ranges (sets of possible values) and supports (values they can take)
  • Discrete probability distributions have probability mass, and those probabilities must sum to 1
  • Continuous probability distributions have probability density under each potential value and the area under the curve must sum to 1
  • The normal (Gaussian) distribution has a range of \(\in (-\infty, \infty)\) and support of all real numbers
  • The Bernoulli distribution has a range of \(\in (0, 1)\) and a support of \((0,1)\)
  • The probability mass function for the Bernoulli distribution is \(P(X=1) = p, P(X=0) = 1-p\)
  • Real numbers are those found on the number line (rational and irrational numbers) but not imaginary; integers are whole numbers that can be positive, negative, or zero; rational numbers can be expressed as ratio of two integers; irrational numbers cannot
  • Empirical data can be used to estimate probability of observing data
  • Probability density or mass functions can be used to estimate the probability of observing data
  • A p-value is the probability of observing your data, or data more extreme, if the null is true

Week 3: Linear models I

Components of linear models

Schedule

  • Quiz (10 min) and discussion (10 min)
  • Plotting bivariate relationships (10 min)
  • Slopes and intercepts (10 min)
  • Deterministic processes (5 min)
  • Functions (10 min)
  • Error and introducing noise (15 min)
  • Stochastic process (15 min)

Learning outcomes

After this lecture, students should know:

  • Linear models have both deterministic and stochastic components
  • Deterministic processes result in the same result every single time
  • Stochastic processes, even when starting from the same conditions, result in different outcomes every time
  • The intercept is the point on the y-axis when the x-axis is fixed at 0; in other words, it is the expected value in the response value when the predictor is 0
  • Centering data makes the intercept term more interpretable because it can then be described as the expected response when the predictor is at the mean
  • The slope parameter describes the relationship between the predictor and response variables; for every one unit increase in the predictor variable, the slope describes the rate of increase in the predictor (i.e., for every one unit increase in x, you get a corresponding change in y)
  • A parameter is a numeric value that we are trying to estimate via modeling, such as the slope parameter or intercept
  • A scatterplot is an easy way to visualize the relationship between two (or more) variables; typically, the x-axis shows the predictor variable and the y-axis shows the response
  • Process error is introduced by the ecological or biological process being studied, and represents true variation (not really ‘error’) around expected responses
  • Observation (or measurement) error describes the variation around the ‘true’ value that is introduced by the researcher because we cannot directly measure true values

Week 4: Linear models II

Evaluating models

Schedule

  • Quiz (10 min) and discussion (10 min)
  • Visual diagnostics of model fit (15 min)
  • Model fit metrics and \(R^2\) (15 min)
  • WS and OOS model evaluation (20 min)
  • Multiple predictors in linear models (10 min)
  • Model comparison (10 min)

Learning outcomes

After this lecture, students should know:

  • Residuals can indicate how good of a fit a model is, because larger residuals indicate worse model fit to the data
  • Model fit describes how well the model describes the data
  • Just because a model is a good fit statistically does not mean it is a good model for the data generating process
  • Models cannot truly be validated, but they can be evaluated based on how well they fit the data
  • Within-sample ‘validation’ checks how well the model describes the data that was used to parameterize the model
  • Out-of-sample evaluates how well a model performs when confronted with new data
  • Overfitting a model means it is mostly driven by the data and likely has poor performance on out-of-sample data
  • Overparameterized models can be overfit, but overfitting and overparameterizing are not synonymous
  • Multicollinearity occurs when multiple predictors covary with one another or are highly correlated and it is difficult to disentangle their effects
  • \(R^2\)
